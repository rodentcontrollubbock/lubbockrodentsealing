What Does a Rodent Exterminator Actually Do on a Service Visit?

Most homeowners have never watched a rodent exterminator work. You call the company, someone shows up with a clipboard and a few traps, they spend some time in your house, and at the end you get an invoice. What actually happened in between is mostly opaque — which makes it hard to tell whether you got a real service visit or just a quick pass-through.

This guide walks through what a professional rodent exterminator should actually be doing during a service visit, how the work breaks down across different visit types, and how to tell whether the service you're getting matches what you're paying for.

There's More Than One Kind of Service Visit

The first thing to understand is that "service visit" isn't a single category. Different visits accomplish different things, and what the technician does depends on which type you've scheduled:

Initial diagnostic visit: The first appointment on a new rodent problem. The technician inspects the property, identifies the species and entry points, and develops a treatment plan. Trap setting usually happens on this visit too.

Trapping check and maintenance visits: Follow-up visits during active trapping, typically every 3 to 7 days. The technician checks all traps, removes any caught rodents, re-baits traps, and assesses whether the population is being effectively reduced.

Exclusion work visits: Dedicated visits for sealing entry points with rodent-grade materials. Often the longest type of visit and usually performed by a different technician or crew than trap maintenance.

Verification visits: After exclusion is complete, a follow-up visit (typically 30 days later) to confirm no new activity and validate the seal.

Ongoing prevention visits: For homeowners on maintenance contracts, periodic visits to inspect for new activity, refresh exterior bait stations, and address any maintenance issues.

A common source of confusion is when homeowners expect a one-and-done service and get billed for what they thought was the whole job — when what they actually got was just the initial visit, with everything else scheduled separately. A good service company explains this upfront.

The Initial Diagnostic Visit — What Should Happen

The first visit is the most important one because everything that follows depends on what's diagnosed here. A real initial visit typically runs 60 to 90 minutes and includes:

The homeowner interview — 10 to 15 minutes of conversation about when activity started, where it's been observed, what's been tried already, household composition, pets, and any related concerns like electrical symptoms or pets behaving strangely. This conversation shapes where the technician focuses.

Exterior perimeter inspection — 20 to 30 minutes walking the entire perimeter of the home, checking foundation gaps, weep holes, utility penetrations, door sweeps, garage seals, dryer vent caps, and any other ground-level entry points. The technician should be photographing or noting each potential issue.

Roofline assessment — 10 to 20 minutes inspecting soffit vents, gable vents, plumbing stacks, roof penetrations, and access routes from trees or utility lines. For homes in roof rat areas of Lubbock, this portion gets extended.

Attic inspection — Actually entering the attic space, not just looking up through the hatch. The technician spends 10 to 20 minutes checking for droppings, runways through insulation, nest sites, gnaw marks on wiring, and visible daylight at penetrations.

Crawl space inspection (pier-and-beam homes) — Similar to attic work but for the space below the floor. Often the dirtiest and slowest part of the visit.

Interior walkthrough — Room by room inspection focused on kitchens, laundry rooms, garages, and any rooms where activity has been reported. The technician should be checking behind appliances, inside lower cabinets, around plumbing penetrations, and along baseboards.

Initial trap deployment — Setting traps in the locations where the inspection identified active travel routes or nest sites. Trap placement is deliberate, not random.

Diagnostic conversation — 10 to 15 minutes explaining findings, species identification, recommended scope, estimated timeline, and pricing. The homeowner should leave this conversation with a clear understanding of what's happening and what's needed.

If your initial visit was 20 minutes and ended with a generic quote, you didn't get a real diagnostic visit.

Trap Maintenance Visits — The Less Glamorous Work

Once trapping is underway, follow-up visits handle the maintenance work that actually drives the population down. A typical trap check visit takes 20 to 45 minutes and covers:

Checking every trap location identified on the initial visit. The technician should know where each trap was placed and check all of them — including the ones in less accessible locations like attic rafters and crawl space joists.

Removing caught rodents and disposing of them safely. This includes proper handling per CDC guidance on rodent handling and disposal, with appropriate PPE and double-bagging of carcasses.

Re-baiting traps that have been triggered or where bait has degraded. Bait selection may change based on what's been working — if a particular bait isn't producing catches, the technician should be willing to try alternatives.

Repositioning traps that aren't producing results. If a trap location has been inactive for several visits while others are catching, the technician may move it to a more active route.

Adding traps if the original placement underestimated the population or if activity is appearing in new locations.

Documenting catches — recording numbers, locations, and species for the case file. This data helps determine when the population has been cleared and exclusion can proceed.

Reporting to the homeowner — a brief update on progress, any concerns, and what to expect on the next visit.

Trap maintenance visits aren't dramatic, but they're where the actual rodent removal happens. Skipping them or rushing them is the most common way otherwise-good services produce poor results.

Exclusion Work Visits — The Building Work

Exclusion is the structural side of rodent control — sealing entry points so the building physically prevents future infestations. This work is dramatically different from trapping and often requires different tools, materials, and skills.

A proper exclusion visit involves:

Confirming the trapping is complete before any sealing happens. Sealing while rodents are still active inside means they die in inaccessible spaces and decompose. The University of California IPM guidance on rats consistently emphasizes this sequence.

Bringing rodent-grade materials to the site: galvanized quarter-inch hardware cloth, sheet metal flashing, copper mesh, stainless steel mesh, heavy-duty door sweeps, and appropriate fasteners. The materials matter — soft fillers like foam or caulk alone aren't exclusion.

Working through the entry point list systematically. Roofline first, then perimeter, then crawl space (if applicable), then any interior penetrations. Each entry point gets the appropriate material installed properly.

Documentation as work progresses — photos of each sealed entry point, notes on what materials were used, and any deviations from the original plan.

Final walkthrough with the homeowner — showing the work done, explaining the materials used, and discussing any areas that may need future attention.

Exclusion work on a typical Lubbock home takes one to three days of crew time, depending on complexity. Visits should be substantive blocks of work, not quick stops between other jobs.

What the Technician Shouldn't Be Doing

A few practices indicate corners being cut:

Spraying chemicals everywhere as a primary treatment. Rodent control isn't a spraying job. Insecticides and general pesticide sprays don't kill rodents and don't prevent rodent activity. A technician who shows up and starts spraying baseboards as if you had an insect problem isn't doing rodent work.

Skipping the attic or crawl space. If the technician declines to enter these spaces — citing time, dust, or any other reason — you're not getting a real inspection. These are where most of the diagnostic information lives.

Using only one type of trap or bait. Rodent control is iterative. A technician committed to one approach without flexibility isn't going to get good results across the variety of situations a real infestation presents.

Pushing poison as a primary solution. Rodenticides have a role in specific situations, but they shouldn't be the default approach. The EPA's guidance on rodenticide use emphasizes that responsible use is limited and that exclusion-based control is preferable for most residential situations.

Refusing to explain the work. A confident technician answers questions directly: what species do we have, where are the entry points, what materials will you use, how long will this take, what's the warranty. Vague or evasive responses are warning signs.

Quoting without diagnosis. A flat quote given before any inspection — or after a five-minute walkthrough — isn't a real quote. It's a sales price designed to capture the job, not a scope based on actual conditions.

What You Should Expect to Receive

After each visit, a professional service should leave you with documentation:

Service report detailing what was inspected, what was found, what was done, and what's planned next.

Trap location map for any active trapping, so you know where the traps are and can keep pets and children away from them.

Photos of significant findings and completed work, especially for exclusion components.

Schedule for next visit with date, expected scope, and any preparation needed.

Updated treatment plan if findings have changed the scope from the original assessment.

If your service visits end with a verbal "see you next week" and a paper receipt, you're getting less documentation than the work warrants.

Choosing Who You Hire

The differences between rodent control providers are real. Picking a service that handles full rodent control Lubbock TX properties need — meaning real diagnostic inspections, proper trap maintenance, complete exclusion work, and substantive documentation — is what determines whether you get permanent resolution or recurring problems.

A few questions to ask before hiring:

How long does your initial inspection take? (Look for 60+ minutes.)

Do you enter attics and crawl spaces during inspections? (The answer should be yes.)

What materials do you use for exclusion work? (Specific rodent-grade materials, not "we'll seal it up.")

How many visits does a typical complete job involve? (Look for trap maintenance plus exclusion plus verification.)

What's your warranty? (Reputable services warranty exclusion work for at least a year, often longer.)

The answers will tell you most of what you need to know about whether this provider is going to do real work or quick work.

The Bottom Line

A rodent exterminator's service visit should be a substantive block of focused work — diagnosis, trapping, or exclusion — with clear documentation and a defined plan for what comes next. If your visits are quick, vague, or ending with vague reassurances rather than specific findings, you're getting less than the work requires.

Real rodent control is a sequence of structured visits that together produce a sealed building with no active population. Quick visits and generic treatments produce repeat business for the company and continuing problems for the homeowner. Understanding what the work should look like protects you from paying for the latter when you needed the former.
